Colonoscopy Guide

The most effective way to prevent colorectal cancer. Here's everything you need to know.

Why It Matters

#3
Most common cancer (both sexes)
90%
Survival when caught early
95%
Preventable through screening

Colonoscopy is unique: it can both detect AND prevent cancer by removing precancerous polyps before they become dangerous.

When to Get Screened

Average Risk

Start at age 45 (updated from 50 in 2021 due to rising rates in younger adults). Repeat every 10 years if normal and no polyps. If polyps found, follow-up is more frequent.

Higher Risk — Start Earlier

Family history: First-degree relative with colon cancer → start at 40, or 10 years before their age at diagnosis (whichever is younger).
Inflammatory bowel disease: Crohn's or ulcerative colitis → screening starts 8 years after diagnosis.
Genetic syndromes: Lynch syndrome, FAP → may start in teens or 20s.

Symptoms to Report

Don't wait for screening age if you have: blood in stool, persistent change in bowel habits, unexplained weight loss, abdominal pain, or iron deficiency anemia.

The Prep (The Hard Part)

Most people say the prep is worse than the procedure. But good prep = better results. Here's what to expect:

Week Before

  • Stop iron supplements, fish oil, blood thinners (as directed)
  • Avoid seeds, nuts, popcorn (if your doctor advises)
  • Fill your prep prescription
  • Stock up on clear liquids

Day Before

  • Clear liquids only—no solid food
  • OK: water, clear broth, tea, coffee (no milk), sports drinks, Jell-O (no red/purple)
  • NOT OK: milk, juice with pulp, anything red/purple
  • Start drinking prep solution as directed (usually afternoon/evening)
  • Stay near a bathroom—you'll need it

Procedure Day

  • Finish any remaining prep (split-dose prep often has morning portion)
  • Nothing by mouth 2-4 hours before (as directed)
  • Arrange a ride—you'll be sedated
  • Wear comfortable, loose clothing

What Happens During

1 Check-In & Prep

You'll change into a gown, answer medical questions, and have an IV placed for sedation.

2 Sedation

Most people receive "twilight sedation"—you're drowsy and comfortable, may drift in and out, and won't remember much. Some centers offer propofol (deeper sedation).

3 The Procedure

A flexible scope with a camera examines your entire colon. Air is used to expand the colon for better viewing. Takes 20-60 minutes depending on findings.

4 Polyp Removal

If polyps are found, they're typically removed immediately (polypectomy). This is painless and why colonoscopy is both diagnostic AND preventive.

5 Recovery

30-60 minutes to wake up. You may feel gassy or crampy (the air). Results are often shared before you leave; polyp biopsy results take days.
