What to Expect During a Colonoscopy

Last reviewed: February 2026 ¬∑ 7 min read

The unknown is often scarier than reality. Here's exactly what happens during a colonoscopy ‚Äî from arrival to going home ‚Äî so you can feel prepared and in control.

üí° The Quick Version

You'll be sedated and won't feel or remember the procedure. The actual colonoscopy takes 30-60 minutes. Most people say the prep was the hardest part, and the procedure itself was "no big deal."

Timeline of Your Visit

‚è∞ Arrival (30-60 minutes before)

Check in at the front desk. You'll complete paperwork, confirm your medical history, and list current medications. A nurse will take vitals (blood pressure, pulse, temperature) and confirm you followed prep instructions.

üè• Pre-Procedure Area

You'll change into a hospital gown and get an IV placed in your arm or hand. This is for fluids and sedation medication. The nurse will review what to expect and answer any last questions. You may meet the anesthesiologist.

üíâ Sedation

Most colonoscopies use "moderate sedation" or "twilight sedation" ‚Äî you're not fully unconscious but very relaxed and drowsy. Many facilities now use propofol for deeper sedation. Either way, you won't feel pain and likely won't remember the procedure.

üî¨ The Procedure (30-60 minutes)

You'll lie on your left side. The doctor inserts a thin, flexible tube (colonoscope) with a camera through the rectum and advances it through your entire colon. Air or CO2 inflates the colon for better visibility. The doctor examines the colon lining carefully as the scope is withdrawn.

üò¥ Recovery (30-60 minutes)

You'll wake up in a recovery area feeling groggy. Nurses monitor you until you're alert enough to go home. You may feel bloated or have mild cramping from the air ‚Äî this passes quickly. Most people feel fine within an hour.

üè† Going Home

Someone must drive you home ‚Äî you cannot drive, take a taxi alone, or use public transit for 24 hours due to sedation effects. The doctor may give preliminary results before you leave. Biopsy or polyp removal results take a few days.

What You'll Feel

During the procedure: Nothing, or at most a vague sense of pressure. Most people remember nothing at all.

After waking up:

  • Groggy, like waking from a nap
  • Bloating or gas ‚Äî pass it freely, it's encouraged
  • Mild cramping that resolves quickly
  • Hungry ‚Äî you've been fasting!

If Polyps Are Found

Polyps are common and usually harmless, but some can become cancer over time. If the doctor finds polyps:

  • They're removed during the procedure ‚Äî No separate surgery needed
  • You won't feel it ‚Äî The colon has no pain receptors for this
  • Samples go to pathology ‚Äî Results in 3-7 days
  • Your next colonoscopy timing ‚Äî Depends on polyp type and number

Rest of the Day

After your colonoscopy:

  • Eat lightly ‚Äî Start with easy foods, gradually return to normal diet
  • Stay home ‚Äî No driving, major decisions, or signing documents for 24 hours
  • Rest ‚Äî Many people nap and feel normal by evening
  • Hydrate ‚Äî You've lost fluids from the prep

When to Call the Doctor

Some symptoms after a colonoscopy require medical attention:

  • Severe abdominal pain (beyond mild cramping)
  • Fever over 101¬∞F
  • Heavy rectal bleeding (more than a tablespoon)
  • Dizziness or weakness that persists

These are rare but should be evaluated promptly.
